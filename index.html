1. The "Phishing" Hook

Scammers often send emails pretending to be Amazon, your bank, or Medicare.

Fact: Legitimate companies will never ask for your password or Social Security Number via email.

Tip: Hover over the link before clicking. If it looks strange (e.g., amazon-help-desk.net instead of amazon.com), do not click it.

Source: FTC Consumer Advice

2. The Padlock Myth

Many people believe the "Padlock" icon in the browser address bar means a site is safe.

Fact: The lock only means the connection is encrypted. Scammers can get padlocks for their fake websites too. Always double-check the website address (URL).

Source: FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center

3. Password Power

Using the same password everywhere is dangerous. If hackers steal it from one site, they try it on your bank account.

Tip: Use a "Passphrase" made of 4 random words (e.g., Blue-Coffee-Truck-Sing). It is harder for computers to crack but easier for you to remember.

Source: CISA (Cybersecurity Agency)

4. Software Updates

Those pop-ups asking you to update your phone or computer are annoying, but critical.

Fact: Updates often contain "patches" for security holes that hackers have discovered. Ignoring them leaves you vulnerable.

Source: StopThinkConnect.org
